The Chemistry of Fart Smell

🥚 Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S)

The classic rotten egg smell. The dominant stinker and the most concentrated compound found in women's farts in the 1998 study. Produced from sulfur-based amino acids in meat and eggs.

🧄 Methanethiol

A garlicky / cabbagey smell. Sulfur-based compound primarily produced when consuming garlic and onions. Not as intense as H₂S but distinctly pungent.

🥦 Dimethyl Sulfide

A milder cooked vegetable smell. Also sulfur-based. Produced by cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts). All three belong to the volatile sulfur compound family.

The Fart Study (1998, Journal Gut)

  • Published in Gut — the leading gastroenterology journal in Europe.
  • 16 participants; two trained "fart judges" scored smell intensity on a 0–8 scale by sniffing collected samples.
  • Finding: Women's farts score higher for odour, driven by higher concentrations of hydrogen sulfide.
  • Men are more likely to pass gas openly; women are better at concealing it.
  • Limitation: predates modern microbiome sequencing — no microbiome analysis was done.

The Microbiome Connection

Why stinky farts are a health signal

Stinky farts indicate the gut microbiome is fermenting too much protein (instead of fibre). This produces toxic compounds like ammonia, associated with increased colorectal cancer risk. Increasing fibre shifts the microbiome toward producing anti-inflammatory short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs).

Beer & Alcohol

Post-drinking rancid gas is a sign of microbiome injury from alcohol. The alcohol disrupts microbial balance, shifting fermentation back toward protein and producing more sulfur compounds.

Red Flags — When to See a Doctor

🔴 Sudden, unexplained change in smell or frequency (no diet change)
🔴 Persistent abdominal cramps, bloating, or discomfort
🔴 Chronic diarrhoea or constipation
🔴 Blood in stool
🔴 Unexplained anaemia or iron deficiency
🔴 Unexplained weight loss
🔴 Fever accompanying GI symptoms

Get Right With Your Gas — Top 10 Fixes

  1. Increase fibre slowly. Your microbiome needs time to adapt. Going straight to a full bean feast causes excessive gas. Build up gradually and let the gut adjust.
  2. Cut back on sulfur-rich foods (meat, eggs, garlic, onions) temporarily if your farts are chronically malodorous. These are the primary H₂S drivers.
  3. Reduce sugar alcohols. Check ingredient labels for sorbitol, xylitol, erythritol, maltitol. These are not absorbed, get fermented, and can also trigger diarrhoea.
  4. Identify your FODMAP triggers. Fructose, lactose, galacto-oligosaccharides (beans), fructans (wheat, garlic, onions), and sugar alcohols are all FODMAPs. Work with a dietitian to pinpoint your specific issue.
  5. Feed your good microbes. Prioritise dietary fibre and resistant starch. Consider targeted microbiome supplements to shift from protein fermentation to fibre fermentation.
  6. Slow down when you eat. Eating too fast leads to sloppy digestion. Slower eating reduces bloating, gas, and even improves metabolism.
  7. Skip gum and carbonated drinks. Both cause you to swallow excess air, which must exit one way or another — either as a belch or from the other end.
  8. Move your body after meals. Even a short walk ("fart walk") can dramatically reduce gas and bloating by stimulating gut motility.
  9. Try digestive enzyme supplements or products like "Discreet" pads that muffle sound and filter odour via activated charcoal — useful situational tools.
  10. Consider dairy. If you suffer with ongoing bloating and gas, trial removing dairy for a week to assess your lactose tolerance level.
