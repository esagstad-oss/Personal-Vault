Why Everything You Know About "Anti-Nutrients" is Wrong

The missing piece both sides of the debate are ignoring

youtube.com/watch?v=KdQ0i82aYN8  |  Duration: ~11 min

Anti-nutrients (lectins, phytates, oxalates) are real — but the debate around them is missing a critical piece: context. Traditional cultures ate massive amounts of these foods for thousands of years without problems, because they always prepared them in ways that neutralised most of the risk. Modern eating removed those preparation steps and stacked high-anti-nutrient foods daily without variety — that is what changed. The answer isn't to eliminate entire food groups; it's to rediscover how those foods were always meant to be eaten.

🥩 Carnivore / Paleo camp

What they get right: the negative effects are real and measurable.
What they miss: preparation, dose, and context change everything.

The missing piece: It's not what you eat — it's how you eat it, and how often. Both sides frame the debate in extremes and miss the nuance that actually matters for health.

The "Stacking Effect" — How Modern Diets Made This Worse

A typical "healthy" modern daily meal plan — every single day:

🥣 Raw oats (water/milk) — breakfast
🥜 Raw nuts — morning snack
🍫 Nut butter + dark chocolate — afternoon
🥬 Spinach smoothie — daily

All technically "healthy" foods. But repeated daily for years without preparation = chronic cumulative anti-nutrient exposure. If your gut is sensitive or you already have mineral deficiencies, this is where symptoms appear — not because these foods are toxic, but because the dose accumulated without the traditional buffers.


🔁 Modern diet amplifiers

  • Same foods every single day (no seasonal variety)
  • No soaking, fermentation, or slow preparation
  • Raw consumption of foods that were always cooked or prepared
  • Instant oats instead of slow-cooked porridge
  • Quick yeast bread instead of sourdough
  • Removed buffering foods (e.g. calcium sources alongside grains)

⚠️ When anti-nutrient reduction makes sense

  • Already low in zinc or magnesium — high phytate intake worsens deficiencies
  • Inflamed gut — certain compounds will irritate it further
  • Eating large amounts of raw spinach daily — oxalate load can reach problematic levels
  • Anyone with a very sensitive gut and repetitive high-exposure diet

Traditional Preparation Methods — The Forgotten Protective Layer

No matter where you look historically, high-anti-nutrient foods were always prepared in one of these ways. None of it was science-based — it was accumulated cooking wisdom passed through generations.

💧

Soaking

Overnight soak activates phytase in grains/legumes, breaking down phytic acid. Beans soaked then simmered for hours.

🦠

Fermenting

Microbes further degrade phytates and lectins. Sourdough vs quick bread — not even in the same universe nutritionally.

🌱

Sprouting

Activates enzymes that break down anti-nutrients in seeds and grains. Increases bioavailability of minerals.

🔥

Slow Cooking

Long heat exposure degrades lectins and enzyme inhibitors. Slow porridge vs instant oats = completely different food.

🥩

Food Pairing

Calcium (e.g. bone broth) buffers phytates in bread. Mineral-rich accompaniments offset anti-nutrient binding effects.

🌿

Seasonal Variety

Traditional diets rotated with availability — avoiding the chronic daily stacking of the same high-anti-nutrient foods year-round.

Hormesis — Small Stress, Big Benefit

🧬 The body evolved alongside these compounds

Humans did not evolve in a sterile food environment. We evolved eating plants with defence compounds. The body expects some exposure. Small amounts of anti-nutrients can act as mild stressors that train the system to become more resilient — similar to exercise stress driving adaptation.

🛡️ Phytic acid as a protector

Phytic acid doesn't only bind essential minerals — it also binds toxic heavy metals: mercury, lead, and cadmium. In small amounts it helps protect the body against these toxins. What the body didn't evolve for is constant unprocessed exposure without traditional buffers.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Soak your oats overnight before eating. Makes them dramatically easier to digest and activates phytase to break down phytic acid. Raw oats with cold water every morning is not how oats were meant to be eaten.
  2. Switch to real sourdough bread. Fermented sourdough and quick supermarket bread are completely different foods in terms of anti-nutrient load and digestibility. Not even the same universe.
  3. Soak beans overnight, then cook them long. Traditional preparation — not a marketing claim. It genuinely changes the biochemical state of the food.
  4. Don't "stack" high-anti-nutrient foods daily without variety. Raw oats + raw nuts + nut butter + spinach smoothie every single day is the pattern that accumulates into problems — not any one of those foods in isolation.
  5. Cook leafy greens (spinach especially) rather than eating them raw in bulk. Raw spinach daily in large amounts can push oxalate load into problematic territory, particularly if you have a sensitive gut.
  6. Pair foods strategically. Calcium-rich foods alongside grain-heavy meals buffer phytate effects. Seasonal variety prevents chronic overexposure to any one anti-nutrient.
  7. Don't eliminate entire food categories. The more you shrink your diet, the more likely you are to develop hidden deficiencies and new intolerances over time. Small adjustments to preparation are almost always preferable to wholesale elimination.
  8. Ask "how" not just "what". In 95% of cases, the problem is not one molecule — it's how foods are grown, prepared, combined, and eaten. Zoom out before concluding a food is inherently problematic.

The Core Reframe

Instead of asking: "Are anti-nutrients good or bad?"

Ask instead: "Are we eating these foods in a way that resembles how our ancestors adapted to them over many generations?"

When you answer that question, anti-nutrients stop looking like hidden poisons — and start looking like manageable compounds you can work with once you know what you're doing.
