How to Build Cycling Workouts

Most People Get This Wrong

Sweet Spot — The Sheltered Side of the Fence

What it is

  • The tipping point between high tempo and low threshold
  • Gets most of the benefits of threshold work without generating as much fatigue and muscular damage
  • Like standing under a fence in wind — sheltered, but you can feel the breeze on your head
  • You can use lactate as fuel in this zone — the body stays aerobic

Common mistake

  • It is NOT meant to feel easy. 3×30 min at 88% FTP is genuinely hard work
  • If sweet spot feels easy, you likely have an FTP increase coming
  • "If sweet spot is hard, your threshold is too high" — this is false
  • By definition sweet spot is strenuous — that's the point

📊
Over/Unders (Under-Overs)

Use sweet spot as the recovery between harder threshold or VO₂ intervals. E.g. 15–30 sec at sweet spot / 15–30 sec at VO₂ repeated for 5–8 min, then full recovery. Forces the body to recover while still producing high power — you can visibly watch the aerobic system re-engage.

🚀
Hard Starts

Sprint hard to create a gap, then settle into sweet spot. Simulates a solo attack. Feels like threshold work. Tip: don't judge the interval by the first 15–20% — you'll be flooded with lactate. It will ease. "It'll get easier" until it does.

⏳
Rest guideline

Sweet spot needs very little rest. At the top of a progression, rest nearly disappears entirely. <10 min between intervals is generous — you can get away with much less.

Threshold — Where the Money Is Made

The Physiology (the exhaust metaphor)

As you use more glycogen, your aerobic motor produces exhaust (hydrogen ions from lactate reprocessing). That exhaust accumulates and creates an acidic environment — the burn. At threshold, you hit the inflection point where exhaust is produced faster than it can be reprocessed. Unlike a car, your body can burn its own exhaust (lactate is "jet fuel") — but the leftover hydrogen is the problem.

Threshold work trains your body to reprocess more exhaust per session — it "gets the memo" and adapts. The strategy: progressively extend interval duration, even by 2 minutes at a time.

FTP ≠ your training anchor. Some athletes should train with a higher or lower power number than their actual maximal lactate steady state. TrainerRoad largely ignores your FTP and instead evaluates your ability independently in each zone — this is why it actually works.
🔁
Over/Unders

E.g. Pick a Guard: 2 min at 90% / 2 min at 105%, repeated × 3 = one set. Extremely race-specific — mimics a 2-person breakaway. Painful but highly effective even early in the season for moving threshold.

📐
Traditional cuts

3×10, 5×10, 2×20. Valid, but overrated as the only format. Short threshold intervals (5×5 min) with limited rest are equally effective and more mentally manageable when you're building the zone.

🛠️
Ramps

Intensity gradually climbs across the interval. Useful for climbing-specific build phases — mimics a finishing climb where effort escalates to the summit.

⏳
Rest guideline

Rest should be 50–100% of your interval time. Example: 10-min interval → 5–10 min rest. Don't try to do an hour on day one — build to it from 6 minutes upward.

VO₂ Max — Peak Aerobic Uptake

The physiology bottleneck

The limiting factor is not usually breathing — it's the muscle's ability to pull oxygen out of the blood. VO₂ max intervals increase capillary density, create more mitochondria, and build more oxygen "receiving docks" at the muscle level. You're training your body to uptake more oxygen at peak effort, delaying the point where you go anaerobic.

⏱️
Traditional: 2–5 min intervals

Start at 106% FTP, build toward 120%. If you can hold VO₂ intervals beyond 5 minutes, your threshold is ready to be bumped. Rest: ~4–5 min max between efforts. "5×5 at well-calibrated VO₂ max" = you've beaten the game.

🔀
On/Offs

E.g. 15–30 sec on / 15–30 sec off, repeated for 10–15 min sets. Mentally easier than sustained intervals. Accumulates "time under tension" at VO₂ even before you can hold 5-minute intervals. You reach peak aerobic uptake later in the set, but you still spend significant time there. Great for base phase introduction.

🌊
Float Sets

Alternate 15–30 sec at VO₂ / 15–30 sec at sweet spot, in sets of 5–8 min. Simulates MTB, cyclocross, and criterium racing perfectly. Very race-specific — the float period keeps you honest aerobically while you recover from the surge.

⚡
Why power targets matter for VO₂

Riding purely by feel produces a spike then a "knife blade drop" — you're training yourself to coalesce to peak uptake rather than sustain it. A power target forces you to hold high output at peak aerobic uptake, which is where the real adaptations happen.

Anaerobic — Neuromuscular Recruitment

The goal

  • Recruit all available muscle fibres simultaneously — fast
  • Like switching from an Atkinson-cycle 4-cylinder to a full V6
  • Spicier attacks, faster acceleration, more explosive power even for endurance athletes
  • Improves how quickly you reach peak power — from 15 sec down to 3–4 pedal strokes

Key rules

  • Amplitude is everything — intervals too close together = power drops = wasted session
  • Give full recovery between sets (much more rest than any other zone)
  • Reserved for later in the season — neuromuscular connections need base to build on
  • Also a skill: gear selection, body position, how you hold the bike all matter

🪜
Ladders / Steps (e.g. "Babos")

Shorter = higher intensity; each step gets slightly longer but slightly lower. Vicious, but effective at varying the stimulus.

⚔️
Attacks

Hard high-intensity hit, then hold threshold / sweet spot / VO₂ for an extended block. Extremely race-specific — simulates a real attack with the follow-through effort.

Key Practical Takeaways

  1. Zones are faders, not switches. There's no binary flip between energy systems. You're always mixing — the zones just describe where the emphasis sits.
  2. Threshold is the foundation of everything. All other training either pushes it up from below (sweet spot/endurance) or pulls it up from above (VO₂/anaerobic).
  3. Don't start threshold training with long intervals. Build from 5–6 minute efforts. Add 2 minutes each time. "Last time you held 6 min, today you hold 8." This is how threshold actually moves.
  4. Sweet spot should feel hard. If it doesn't, expect an FTP increase notification. It's strenuous by design — 3×30 min at 88% FTP is serious work.
  5. Hard starts are underutilised. Sprint to open a gap, settle into sweet spot or threshold. Teaches your body to recover aerobically after a maximal effort — essential race skill.
  6. Don't judge interval difficulty in the first 15–20%. You're flooded with lactate. Your body will catch up. Be a goldfish — forget the start and reassess later.
  7. Use on/offs as your VO₂ max gateway. They accumulate "time under tension" at VO₂ before you can sustain full intervals. Mentally easier. Introduce them toward the end of base phase.
  8. Anaerobic work belongs late in the season for most athletes. You need the aerobic base first. Reserve it for late build and race-specific phases.
  9. FTP is a rough guide, not gospel. Some athletes train better above or below their FTP-derived zones. The best coaches decouple athletes from their FTP number — TrainerRoad evaluates each zone independently.
  10. Consistency compounds. You can train your body to be good at ANY zone — you just have to meet yourself where you are and achieve progressive overload. "I'm not good at threshold" usually just means "I've never trained threshold."
